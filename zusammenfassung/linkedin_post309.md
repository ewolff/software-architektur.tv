# 🏛️ Rethinking Software Organizations: What Anarchy Can Teach Us About Self-Organization

Ever felt stuck in endless approval chains? Watched your "autonomous team" ask 17 layers of architects before shipping code? You're not alone — and there might be lessons from an unexpected source: anarchist organizational theory.

In this episode, I spoke with **Andrew Harmel-Law** (author of O'Reilly's "Facilitating Software Architecture") about how anarchistic principles — **voluntary participation, temporary structures, functional teams, and small-scale design** — could revolutionize how we build software.

## Key Takeaways:

✨ **Anarchy ≠ Chaos**: It's actually about self-organization, intentional structure, and devolved authority (not delegation)

🔄 **Temporary Doesn't Mean Chaotic**: Like the Quakers' 3-year rule, rotating roles prevents gatekeeping and spreads knowledge—building team resilience over individual heroes

📦 **Small Teams Scale Differently**: Netflix didn't add hierarchy; they added layers of different-scoped teams. Think **emerging federations**, not org charts

🔐 **Trust Over Control**: From Andrew's 90-person self-managing team to Netflix's minimal expense policies—what happens when you trust people?

🤖 **The AI Question**: Will LLMs force more autonomy or enable surveillance? That depends on *conscious choice*, not blind adoption
